How to Write a
Literature Review

A literature review has 8 sections, each with a specific purpose. Follow this structure exactly — journals and reviewers expect it. Your total paper should be 2,500–3,500 words excluding references. Write in clear, formal English. Every claim must be backed by a citation.

Section 01 Title 1 line

The title is the first thing editors and reviewers read — make it specific, academic, and descriptive.

What to write

  • State what you are studying (microplastic contamination)
  • State where (food and water sources)
  • State context (Nepal / public health)
  • Include the study type (literature review)
✦ Recommended Title

"Microplastic Contamination in Food and Water Sources of Nepal: A Literature Review and Public Health Risk Assessment"

Section 02 Abstract 200–250 words

A self-contained summary of the entire paper. Write this LAST — after everything else is done.

4 paragraphs — one sentence each minimum

  • Background: Why microplastics are a global health concern
  • Objective: What this review aims to examine
  • Methods: How you searched for literature (PubMed, Google Scholar, keywords used)
  • Findings: Main health risks identified
  • Conclusion: What Nepal needs to do — research gaps and recommendations
💡

Write the abstract last. It is a summary — you cannot summarize what you haven't written yet.

Section 03 Introduction 400–500 words

Hook the reader. Establish why this topic matters globally and specifically for Nepal.

Write in this exact order

  • Hook sentence: A striking global fact about plastic pollution or microplastics
  • Define microplastics — what they are, size classification (<5mm), types (primary vs secondary)
  • Global scale — how widespread contamination is in food and water worldwide
  • Why Nepal is specifically vulnerable — geography, waste management, water sources
  • The research gap — very little Nepal-specific data exists
  • Purpose statement — what this review does and why it matters
✦ Example Hook

"Microplastics have been detected in every corner of the globe — from the deepest ocean trenches to Arctic snow — and emerging evidence now confirms their presence in the human food supply, raising urgent concerns for public health worldwide."

💡

End the introduction with a clear purpose statement: "This review aims to synthesize existing literature on microplastic contamination in food and water, assess associated health risks, and highlight implications for Nepal."

Section 04 Methods / Search Strategy 150–200 words

Explains HOW you found your sources. Short but essential — shows your review is systematic, not random.

What to include

  • Databases searched: PubMed, Google Scholar, WHO reports, NHRC Nepal
  • Keywords used: "microplastics", "food contamination", "drinking water", "human health", "South Asia", "Nepal"
  • Date range of papers included (e.g. 2015–2025)
  • Language: English only
  • Inclusion criteria: peer-reviewed studies, WHO/UN reports
  • Exclusion criteria: non-English, opinion pieces without data
  • Total papers reviewed: state the number (aim for 30+)
✦ Example Sentence

"A systematic search was conducted across PubMed, Google Scholar, and WHO databases using the keywords 'microplastics', 'food contamination', 'drinking water safety', and 'public health Nepal', covering publications from 2015 to 2025."

Section 05 Sources & Pathways of Microplastics 500–600 words

Where do microplastics come from and how do they enter food and water? This is your environmental science section — where your co-author's PhD in Botany adds real value.

Cover these subsections

  • In drinking water: Rivers, groundwater, tap water, bottled water contamination studies
  • In food: Seafood, salt, honey, packaged foods, agricultural soil contamination
  • Atmospheric deposition: Microplastics in air settling onto crops and open water
  • Plastic packaging: Migration from containers into food and beverages
  • Nepal-specific sources: Fewa Lake, Bagmati River, poor waste management, plastic use in agriculture
💡

Mention Fewa Lake in Pokhara specifically — documented pollution + your local connection makes this section uniquely credible.

Section 06 Health Effects of Microplastic Exposure 600–700 words

This is your most important section as a medicine applicant. Go deep into the clinical and physiological impacts.

Cover each system with citations

  • Gastrointestinal effects: Gut microbiome disruption, intestinal inflammation, barrier dysfunction
  • Endocrine disruption: Chemical leaching (BPA, phthalates) affecting hormones
  • Immune system: Inflammatory response, oxidative stress, immune activation
  • Cardiovascular: Microplastics detected in human blood and arterial plaques (cite recent 2023–24 studies)
  • Carcinogenic potential: Association with colorectal cancer, emerging evidence
  • Reproductive health: Effects on fertility — detected in placenta and breast milk
  • Vulnerable populations: Children, pregnant women, elderly — higher risk
✦ Strong Opening Line for This Section

"Once ingested, microplastics and their associated chemical additives may exert toxic effects across multiple organ systems, with emerging clinical evidence linking chronic exposure to gastrointestinal inflammation, endocrine disruption, and cardiovascular pathology."

💡

Search PubMed for "microplastics human blood 2023" and "microplastics placenta" — these are landmark recent papers that will make your review feel very current and credible.

Section 07 Nepal-Specific Context 400–500 words

This section is what makes your paper unique. No other review will have this — it's your original contribution.

What to write here

  • Plastic waste situation: Nepal generates significant plastic waste with limited recycling infrastructure
  • Water sources at risk: Fewa Lake (Pokhara), Bagmati River, glacial meltwater — all documented for pollution
  • Food safety gaps: Street food culture, open-air markets, plastic packaging of staple foods
  • Healthcare readiness: Nepal lacks systematic microplastic monitoring or policy framework
  • Research gap: Clearly state — very few Nepal-specific microplastic studies exist. This is the gap your paper addresses.
  • Policy context: Nepal's plastic ban attempts and their limitations
💡

Even if you cannot find many Nepal-specific papers, that IS your finding — the absence of data is itself a research gap worth documenting.

Section 08 Discussion & Conclusion 400–500 words

Synthesize everything. What does it all mean? What should Nepal do? End strong.

Discussion — write in this flow

  • Summarize the key findings from your review — don't repeat, synthesize
  • Discuss the limitations of existing studies (small sample sizes, lab conditions vs real-world, Nepal data gaps)
  • Discuss what this means for Nepal's public health — who is most at risk, what diseases could increase
  • Policy recommendations — what Nepal's government and health sector should do

Conclusion — 1 strong paragraph

  • Restate the severity of microplastic contamination as a public health issue
  • Emphasize the critical need for Nepal-specific research
  • Call for national monitoring systems, policy action, and public health awareness
  • End with a forward-looking sentence about the future of this research
✦ Example Closing Sentence

"As the global burden of microplastic contamination continues to grow, Nepal must urgently invest in national surveillance, evidence-based policy, and interdisciplinary research to protect the health of its population from this emerging environmental threat."

✦ ✦ ✦
Section 09 References 25–35 citations

Use Vancouver style — the standard for medical journals. Number citations in order of appearance in the text.

Vancouver Format

Author AB, Author CD. Title of article. Journal Name. Year;Volume(Issue):Pages.

Example

Smith J, Patel R. Microplastics in drinking water. Environ Health. 2022;21(3):45–52.

Where to Find Sources

PubMed · Google Scholar · WHO reports · NHRC Nepal · Lancet · Nature

Use Zotero (free app) to manage references automatically — saves hours of formatting work.

Must Know 01 What is "et al." ?

You will use this constantly while writing. Understand it perfectly.

Definition

"et al." is Latin for "and others." When a research paper has 3 or more authors, you only write the first author's last name and add "et al." instead of listing everyone.

When to use it

  • 1 author: Smith (2020)
  • 2 authors: Smith and Jones (2020)
  • 3 or more authors: Smith et al. (2020) ← use et al.

In text vs In reference list

  • In your text: Deng et al. (2020) reported oxidative stress following microplastic exposure.
  • In your reference list: Write ALL authors in full — Deng Y, Wang L, Zhang B, Chen W. Title. Journal. 2020;18(2):112–119.
💡

et al. is only a shortcut for in-text citations. Your reference list at the end must always have every author's full name.

Must Know 02 Minimum Sources Required

How many references do you actually need?

Source count guide

  • Absolute minimum: 20 sources — below this looks weak
  • Recommended: 25–30 sources — solid and credible
  • Sweet spot for you: 28 sources — thorough without being overwhelming for a student paper

Where to find your 28 sources

  • PubMed — pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov — most credible medical database
  • Google Scholar — scholar.google.com — broad academic search
  • WHO reports — who.int — international health authority
  • NHRC Nepal — nhrc.gov.np — Nepal-specific health data
  • Lancet / Nature / BMJ — top tier journals, very credible
💡

Use Zotero (free app) to collect and format all references automatically. It saves hours of manual work.

Must Know 03 What a Real Paragraph Looks Like

This is exactly how every paragraph in your paper should be structured. Study this carefully.

Sample paragraph — Health Effects section

✦ Sample Paragraph

Several studies have demonstrated that microplastic ingestion poses significant risks to the human gastrointestinal system. Lu et al. (2018) found that polystyrene microplastics caused intestinal inflammation and disrupted gut microbiome diversity in animal models. Similarly, Deng et al. (2020) reported oxidative stress and metabolic disturbances following chronic microplastic exposure. More recently, Leslie et al. (2022) made the landmark discovery of microplastic particles in human blood samples, suggesting systemic circulation beyond the gut. Collectively, these findings indicate that microplastic exposure may contribute to chronic inflammatory conditions, with implications for colorectal health and beyond.

What happened in that paragraph — breakdown

  • Sentence 1: Your own opening claim — what this paragraph is about
  • Sentence 2: Lu et al. finding — summarized in your own words + cited
  • Sentence 3: Deng et al. finding — linked with "Similarly"
  • Sentence 4: Leslie et al. finding — linked with "More recently"
  • Sentence 5: YOUR synthesis — "Collectively these findings indicate..." — this is your original contribution
💡

Every paragraph = opening claim + 2–3 cited studies + your own synthesis sentence. That last sentence is what shows your intelligence as a reviewer.

Must Know 04 Linking Words to Use

These words connect your paragraphs and studies smoothly. Use them constantly — they make your writing flow like a real academic paper.

To Add Similar Findings

Similarly · Furthermore · In addition · Consistently · Likewise · Also · Moreover

To Show Contrast

However · In contrast · Despite this · Nevertheless · On the other hand · Conversely

To Conclude / Synthesize

Collectively · Overall · Taken together · These findings suggest · In summary · Thus · Therefore

To Show Time / Sequence

More recently · Subsequently · Earlier studies · Building on this · Following this

Must Know 05 How Much AI Can You Use?

Critical to understand — using AI wrong can get your paper rejected by the journal entirely.

The golden rule

You write every sentence. AI only polishes. Journals including JNMA now use AI detection tools. A fully AI-written paper gets desk rejected immediately — never even reviewed.

What AI is allowed for

  • ✅ Finding paper ideas and structure — fine
  • ✅ Explaining complex medical terms to you — fine
  • ✅ Checking your grammar after YOU write — fine
  • ✅ Paraphrasing one sentence you already wrote — carefully
  • ❌ Writing full paragraphs for you — not allowed
  • ❌ Writing entire sections — not allowed
  • ❌ Generating citations — never, always verify manually on PubMed
💡

Your authentic student voice + real research = credible paper. A slightly imperfect but genuine paper beats perfectly polished AI text every time.

✦ Before You Submit — Checklist

Title is specific, descriptive, and includes "literature review"
Abstract written last — covers background, methods, findings, conclusion
Introduction ends with a clear purpose statement
Methods section lists databases, keywords, date range, inclusion/exclusion criteria
Health effects section covers at least 4 body systems with citations
Nepal-specific context section includes Fewa Lake / local data
Discussion identifies research gaps and gives policy recommendations
Minimum 25 references in Vancouver format
Total word count is between 2,500–3,500 words
Co-author (Biology PhD teacher) has reviewed and approved the paper
Submitted to target journal — JNMA or Nepal Medical College Journal
